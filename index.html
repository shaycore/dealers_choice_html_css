Korean Folk Tales

Introduction

Korean Folklore, originating from Buddhism, Shamanism, and Confucianism, was and remains a big part of Korean culture.

Common motifs in Korean Folklore include:

  • Supernatural Beings (Such as Dokkaebi)
  • Animals such as Tigers
  • Mountainsides and Landscapes
  • Gender Conflict
  • Birth and Fertility
  • Filial piety
  • Song and Dance
  • Nature and Farming

Talchum

Talchum is a folk song and dance; it is a drama wherein which the performers don traditional masks (as seen in the image to the right) and clothes.

A traditional Talchum consists of many stories that are strung together through different acts, in a vignette style.

The Story of Shimcheong

There are many versions of the Tale of Shimcheong, but this well-known Korean folk tale follows generally the same story beats. This example will pull from one of many interpretations of this folk tale.

Long, long ago, there was a poor family that lived in the Peach Blossom Village. After the death of his wife, Blind Man Sim was the single father of his young daughter, Sim Cheong.

A Buddhist Monk told Blind Man Sim that by offering 300 bags of rice to Buddha, he would be able to see again.

However, he does not have the necessary amount. To pay for the bags, Sim Cheong sacrifices herself to Sea Merchants, who throw her into the water as a sacrifice to the Indang Sea.

What meets her after she throws herself into the stormy sea is not death, but the Jade Emperor, who takes her to the Dragon Palace of the Dragon King.

Eventually, she is sent back to the surface inside of a lotus flower, only to be seen by the Chinese Emperor of the Song Dynasty, who decides to take her as his wife.

As an Empress, Sim Cheong holds a feast for the blind and is able to reunite with her father, who is overjoyed and thus regains his eyesight.
